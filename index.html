The forced seller test

Before you take on this leverage play, answer the only question that matters: will this make you a forced seller under stress?

The WealthOS thesis
"The first rule of investing is never be a forced seller."
Everything in this tool serves that single rule.
Step 1 — The collateral
What you're borrowing against. This is the asset you keep — it stays in your name and keeps compounding.
Different assets have different volatility and call thresholds.
$
Current market value of the asset.
Step 2 — Your LTV ceilings
Doctrine is a SIZING rule — it governs what you choose to borrow at execution. Once you've executed within your doctrine, market movement may push your LTV around. That's not a doctrine violation — that's gravity. Only margin call thresholds determine forced selling.
%
The max LTV you've committed to when SIZING a play. Your rule for how much to borrow — not a leash that follows the asset around. At 25% LTV, you survive ~64% drawdown before margin call.
%
Where your lender forces action. This is the only line that creates a forced sale. Leave blank to use the typical default for this asset type.
Step 3 — The loan
Loan amount auto-calculates from your doctrine ceiling. Override if you want to model a smaller play.

%
Annual rate on the loan. Determines how much liquidity you need to service it.
Step 4 — Your liquidity stack
All capital you can access within 7 days WITHOUT selling the collateral or the asset you're acquiring. This is what keeps you from being a forced seller when stress hits.
$
What counts: Cash, T-bills, money market, undrawn lines of credit you've already arranged, HELOC capacity you can tap immediately, asset-backed borrowing capacity outside this play. What doesn't count: Retirement accounts you'd pay penalties on, illiquid assets, theoretical loans you haven't pre-arranged, anything that requires more than 7 days. Only count liquidity that's genuinely accessible within a week — banks revoke lines, markets freeze, theoretical access disappears when you need it.
